KEYWORDS
Comparative genomics is a field of biological research in which one compares the genomic sequences and characteristics of multiple organisms in order to find out the similarity and difference between them.

Homologs = Orthologs ∪ Paralogs

https://bioinformaticshome.com/bioinformatics_tutorials/sequence_alignment/homology.html

One of the most fundamental and important concepts in comparative genomics is HOMOLOGY.
Homologous genes = genes that share a common ancestor
Orthologs arise from a common ancester gene after a speciation event, leading to similar genes (hence potentially conserved function)
While, the result of a duplication event are paralogs that can evolve new functions.
These are some additional keywords that I will explain later in the presentation. Just one quick note: during my talk, I will use the term “gene” to mean “protein”. So, when I say gene, I am referring only to protein-coding genes.
QUESTION

What genetic mechanisms underlie this fish’s
unique phenotype compared to related species?

Let start with a scientific question. So, we observed something interest about an organism, so we ask ourselve, what make this organism different than the others (closely related species)? Which genetic mechanisms give this species such a unique phenotype? More precise, which regulatory changes contribute to this observation, or, which genes or proteins are responsible for this phenotype, etc.
SEQUENCING

The next step we should do is to take a sample of the organism and do the sequencing. Why:
- With the current technique, it is the cheapest (maximal info per dolar) and quickest way to have an overview of the novelty of the organism such as: identify gene gain/loss, gene family expansion, unique or fast-evolving genes, regulatory regions, metabolic pathways from the gene set, so on and so forth
- And therefore we can better formulate our hypothesis for the downstream analyses

This is a standard pipeline of any sequencing project (actually whole genome sequecing).
First, we cut the original genome into pieces to put into the sequencing machine and get out milions of sequence reads
Then, we assemble those reads into larger fragments called contigs and scaffolds
If we are lucky, we can reconstruct the whole chromosome or original genome
After that, we do the genome annotation to predict genes and gene products from the assembly

This assembly and the gene set or protein set are the fundamental data for studying our species of interest.
Because of that, the quality of these data is very important and can affect all the conclusion of our downstream analyses
QUALITY CONTROL
Therefore, the first step before doing any further studies is to check for the quality of our assembly and gene annotation
There are some basic metrics for evaluating the quality of an assembly, such as N50 length, the genome coverage, BUSCO completeness, etc.
And from this step come our BIONF tools into the game. (N50: minimum contig length required to cover 50 percent of the assembled genome sequence => Assembly Contiguity
Assembled genome coverage of 90%: assembly contains sequences representing approximately 90% of the organism’s total genome size => assess the completeness of the draft genome)

Is my genome "clean"?

Contamination

Horizontal gene transfer

Once we have a genome assembly, one important question is whether it is clean. Some sequences may come from contamination—DNA from other organisms that accidentally got into the sample—while others may result from horizontal gene transfer, meaning genes that truly became part of the genome. Both types of these foreign sequences can confuse our interpretation. For example, finding a chicken gene in a fish genome does not mean fish are related to chickens; it could simply be contamination because the TA was eating chicken while doing the sequencing...
To find out such contamination genes, one of my colleague, Freya, has developed a tool called taXaminer


Freya Arthen (2022), Master thesis

https://github.com/BIONF/taXaminer

taXaminer will have the assembly data as the main input and take into account 16 different characters of the assembly and the gene set to cluster the similar genes together such as the read coverage, the GC content, the gene length, the gene position on the contigs, etc.
It uses the Principal component analysis (PCA) to reduce the dimention of those characters into 2 or 3 dimentions in order to be easy to show and intepret with 2D or 3D plot

The tool combines this clustering and the taxonomic assignment of the gene set to distinguish between HGT and contamination. The main cloud contains sequences from our species. While the points outside normally belong to short contigs and have low read coverage. If those sequences are assigned to other species than our target species, they are candidates for contaminations. While the foreign sequences that are clustered in the main cloud could be because of HGT events because they integrated into the genome and have therefore the same characteristics of other genes of our species.

Is my gene set complete? And how complete is it?

Another question we can ask is: Is my predicted gene set complete? To answer this, we first check whether the number of predicted genes is roughly what we expect. For example: Fungal species usually have 8,000–12,000 genes; typical vertebrates have 20,000–25,000 genes; duplicated fish lineages, like salmonids, can have 40,000–50,000 genes
Next, we also want to see if the core genes—the essential genes found in all members of a taxonomic group—are present in our gene set. A commonly used tool for this is BUSCO. BUSCO can tell us: How many core genes are present (completeness), Whether they are complete or only partially predicted (fragmentation). But gene set assessment is not just about quantity. We also care about quality — how accurate and reliable our predicted genes are.
To adress this question, we have developed fCAT

Tran & Ebersberger (2022), https://doi.org/10.7490/f1000research.1119126.1

https://github.com/BIONF/fCAT

First, we choose the taxonomic clade we are interested in and select representative species. Based on these, fCAT identifies core genes, which are orthologs shared by all or most species in the clade.
Next, fCAT searches for these orthologs in the species of interest using fDOG. It then evaluates the predicted genes in two ways: by sequence length, to determine whether they are complete or fragmented, and by protein domain architecture, to assess whether they are structurally and functionally similar.
The length-based evaluation is similar to BUSCO, while the domain-based analysis is the key novel feature of fCAT.

By comparing protein domain architectures, we can gain deeper insight into the quality of a genome annotation. Here, I show two examples from the gene set of this fish.
In the example on the left, we identified an ortholog that was classified as fragmented. When we compared it to orthologs from other species, we observed that it lacks the BTB and BACK domains at the N-terminus. By inspecting the genome assembly, we found a gene located directly upstream that contains exactly these two missing domains. This suggests a gene fission event in Trachinus draco, where the original gene was split into two separate parts.
In the second example, we identified an ortholog that was classified as complete based on sequence length. However, the domain-based analysis classified it as dissimilar. This was due to two small sequence insertions in the assembly that disrupted the protein domains and could potentially affect or even destroy the protein’s function.
Orthology search

After validating the genome assembly and annotation, we can study about the characteristics of our species. In this example, we check for the ability to synthesize DHA of our new fish.
For that, we search through literatures for the same study in other closely related organisms to get the reference data.

Feature-aware directed (targeted) ortholog search

Here, the main enzymes involved in the DHA biosynthesis pathway are the FAD and ELOVL proteins. Our next step is to search for these genes — or, more precisely, their orthologs — in the species of interest.
Most existing ortholog search tools rely on an all-versus-all approach, meaning they compare entire gene sets across species to identify orthologs. This strategy is very time-consuming, especially when we are only interested in a small subset of genes.
To address this, we developed a targeted ortholog search approach called fDOG.

FAD: Flavin adenine dinucleotide => enzyme for fatty acid desaturation (remove 2 Hydrogen atoms to create carbon/carbon double bond)
ELOVL: Elongase of very long-chain fatty acid

Tran et al. (2025), https://doi.org/10.1093/molbev/msaf120

https://github.com/BIONF/fDOG

Starting with a seed protein — for example, an FAD or ELOVL from the previous slide — fDOG iteratively searches for orthologs in a set of so-called core taxa. After each iteration, newly identified orthologs are added to the core ortholog group, which is then used to build a profile HMM for the core group. A profile HMM is a statistical summary derived from a multiple sequence alignment that represents a protein family and allows sensitive detection of homologous sequences.
This core compilation step continues until a predefined number of orthologs is reached. At that point, the iteration stops and the final profile HMM is constructed.
Next, fDOG uses this profile HMM to search for similar sequences in the species of interest. Candidate hits are then evaluated using a reciprocal criterion. Sequences that pass this test are accepted as orthologs.
Finally, fDOG generates an output to show the presence or absence of all orthologs across different taxa. This is called the phylogenetic profile of the seed protein.
Phylogenetic profile analysis

Phylogenetic profile

Presence of orthologs ⇒ likely presence of the function

A phylogenetic profile of a gene, as I mentioned earlier, is originally defined as the presence or absence of that gene across a set of species.
The presence of an ortholog in a species usually indicates that the function of the gene is also present in that organism.

Phylogenetic profile

Similar profiles ⇒ functional association

We can also compare the phylogenetic profiles of different genes. If two genes have similar profiles, meaning they are present and absent in the same species, they are likely to work together or be functionally related.
This approach is called phylogenetic profiling.

Visualization and exploration of phylogenetic profiles


Tran et al. (2018), https://doi.org/10.1093/bioinformatics/bty225

Tran & Ebersberger (2025), https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2504.19710

The phylogenetic profiles generated from fDOG is not simply binary. They also contain additional information between the orthologs besides their presence / absence patterns.
Visualizing and exploring these more complex profiles in an Excel table is often not practical. Therefore we have developed the PhyloProfile tool to work with these rich phylogenetic profiles.

https://github.com/BIONF/PhyloProfile

PhyloProfile is an interactive R-Shiny app for visualizing phylogenetic profiles of thousands of genes across thousands of species.
Here you see the main window with the profile plot. The x-axis represents the taxa, and the y-axis represents the genes. Each dot indicates the presence of an ortholog for the corresponding taxon. The color of the dot or the background represents additional information; in this example, it shows protein domain architecture similarity, which you already have heard about from the last slide.
The tool uses the NCBI taxonomy database to order taxa according to their taxonomic distance from the species of interest. The plot can be dynamically adjusted to display profiles at different taxonomic levels, from strain and species up to kingdom or superkingdom.
With PhyloProfile, we can perform downstream analyses of phylogenetic profiles, such as: (1) Estimating the evolutionary age of genes using the last common ancestor algorithm (2) Identifying the core gene set for phylogenetic tree reconstruction (3) Visualizing the distribution of additional information, like domain similarity (4) Clustering profiles for functional association studies (phylogenetic profiling) (5) Or dynamically filtering data for further analysis
More importantly, PhyloProfile allows us to scale comparative studies from analyzing entire gene sets across thousands of taxa down to pairwise comparisons between two orthologs, helping to investigate differences in their domain architecture.
Protein feature comparison

Protein feature architecture comparison

You’ve heard about protein architecture similarity several times. Simply put, proteins are made of features or domains—for example, conserved domains from PFAM or SMART, low-complexity regions, or transmembrane domains.
By comparing these features between proteins, we can learn about the functional conservation or diversitification of those proteins. Differences in the domain architectures can also point to technical issues in gene prediction, as we have seen in the example of fCAT.

MFS_1: Major facilitator superfamily; transmembrane transporters; target a wide spectrum of substrates, including ions, carbohydrates, lipids, amino acids and peptides, nucleosides and other small molecules
MFS_2: Sugar transport protein
Alpha_L_AF_C: Alpha-L-arabinofuranosidase C-terminus; catalyses the hydrolysis of alpha-L-arabinofuranosidic linkages in L-arabinose-containing polysaccharides
TMHMM: a section of a protein that contains a single helix and is highly hydrophobic, allowing it to anchor the protein within the cell membrane
SEG: regions in protein sequences that differ from the composition and complexity of most proteins; normally associated with globular structure

Feature architecture similarity (FAS)

To systematically compare protein architectures—rather than just looking at domain plots, we developed a metric called the FAS score. The FAS scores are calculated using the greedyFAS tool, which was developed by Julian.

Dosch et al. (2018), https://doi.org/10.1093/bioinformatics/btad226

https://github.com/BIONF/FAS

The FAS score measures how similar two protein architectures are, ranging from 0 (completely different) to 1 (identical). FAS tool compares the annotated features and penalizes missing features based on which protein is the reference.
For example, if A is the reference, missing domains in B lower the score, while in the reverse comparison the score can be higher because all B’s domains are found in A.
